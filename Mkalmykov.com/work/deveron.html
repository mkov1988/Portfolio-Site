Overview

Abstract

Through a year-long initiative, we increased revenue by $18M by retaining and increasing our labor pool through revolutionizing their (Soil Collectors') experience. This led to enhanced job satisfaction, improved productivity, and ensured business growth and stability.

Trust

Earned, Not Given

Trust is the cornerstone of every successful organization. It's the invisible glue that binds leaders and their teams. It's the silent whisper that says, "We can do this together." Consider an organization with budding potential, but its design maturity is still in its infancy.

Before one can be effective in any organization, they need the trust of senior leadership. This organization's design maturity was still in its infancy. This case study starts 6 months before the actual initiative by gaining solid UX wins for all the other business heads to build trust in UX methodologies.

For instance, take the Sales leader struggling with shared spaces and loud calls. The solution? A half-day design thinking workshop that paves the way for phone booths and modular office furniture. Then there's the Data leader, who was wrestling with feedback loops. By implementing UXR strategies to facilitate better feedback mechanisms, we were able to enhance the flow of information. Consider the Operations leader. Lost in the fog of timing and criteria of point collection, they crave transparency. A well-structured communication and reporting protocol sweeps away the mist, granting them the clarity they seek.

In all of these, lies the linchpin. The one who sees the chaos, the gaps, the friction, and instead of turning back, they lean in. They embrace the challenges, forge connections, and build bridges where there were once walls. Great companies don't succeed on titles or hierarchy, but on collaboration and mutual respect. It's about reaching across departments, breaking down silos, and working together for a common goal.

The Challenge

Deveron, an emerging agricultural organization, faced a struggle with fulfilling contracts due to a shortage of Soil Collectors. The initial assumption was to increase wages, but our research indicated that the issue was more complex.

Research

Methodology

Our research involved onsite research with Soil Collectors and stakeholders. Through interviews and discussions, we were able to understand their needs, motivations, and challenges. It was revealed that the Soil Collectors were not primarily motivated by wages. In fact, they could make more money working as a barista at Starbucks. What truly motivated them was their passion for agriculture. They saw soil collection as a stepping stone towards a successful career in agriculture. They also expressed a desire to understand more about the purpose and use of the soil they were collecting.

We also conducted stakeholder interviews which revealed a significant disconnect between the expectations set during onboarding and the reality of the job. Many Soil Collectors did not realize the job would require them to be away from home for extended periods, that it wouldn't be a typical 9-5 job, and that it wouldn't necessarily guarantee 40 paid hours per week. This insight was crucial in informing our approach to problem-solving during the subsequent design thinking workshop.

Design Thinking Workshop

Workshop Approach

Having gathered a wealth of knowledge from our research, we applied these insights within the confines of our Design Thinking Workshop. This involved the Soil Collectors, stakeholders, and our design team, using several techniques such as structured brainstorming, HMW (How Might We) problem framing, affinity diagramming, dot voting, empathy mapping, journey mapping, and the creation of user personas.

Empathy Mapping

Through the process of empathy mapping, we delved deep into the psyche of the Soil Collectors, capturing their experiences, attitudes, and motivations, allowing us to comprehend the various textures of their work and discern their true needs and frustrations.

User Personas and Journey Mapping

We charted the course of a Soil Collector's workday by journey mapping, from the initial contact to they left, and even after! This aided us in pinpointing their trials and crafting tailored solutions. User personas were born, fictional characters created from our user research, representing the diverse user types that we desperately needed to retain.

Problem Framing and Ideation

By applying the HMW problem framing, we succinctly and clearly defined the problem, setting the stage for ideation. The process of brainstorming unfurled a panoply of ideas, sparking creativity and innovation. Affinity diagramming served to group these ideas, facilitating the identification of patterns and themes. The team then democratically selected the most compelling solutions using dot voting.

Solutions

Based on the insights gathered from the research and the design thinking workshop, we developed a dozen targeted solutions to address the key challenges. Here are a few:

Career path

One of the primary solutions was the development of a career path for Soil Collectors within the organization. This career path provided Soil Collectors with a clear progression plan, allowing them to visualize their future within the organization. It also addressed their desire for a meaningful career in agriculture.

Production Pay

Another significant change was the modification of the payment structure. A Production pay model was introduced, where Soil Collectors were paid based on the amount of soil points they collected. This model was more aligned with the Soil Collectors' expectations and provided a fairer remuneration system.

Employee Portal

Lastly, to increase transparency and allow Soil Collectors to track their pay and performance, an employee portal was created. Here, Soil Collectors could see how the soil they collected was being used and compare their performance with others.
